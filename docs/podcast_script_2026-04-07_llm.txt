Metaproteomics is rapidly becoming a cornerstone for understanding how microbial communities function in health and disease. By detecting and quantifying thousands of proteins from complex microbiomes, researchers can move beyond who is present to what they are actually doing. But the accuracy of these measurements depends critically on the quantification method used. A new study from bioRxiv challenges a widely adopted practice in the field and shows that a simple change can dramatically improve the reliability of metaproteomic data.

The core issue centers on how proteins are quantified in data-dependent acquisition metaproteomics. Two main approaches dominate: MS1-based area under the curve, or AUC, and MS2-based peptide spectral counts, or SpC. AUC measures the integrated signal of peptide ions across a chromatographic run, while SpC simply counts how many times a peptide's spectrum is observed. Both have trade-offs, but a third factor has crept into standard workflows—Match Between Runs, or MBR.

MBR is designed to reduce data sparsity by transferring peptide identifications from one sample to another based on accurate mass and retention time matching. It sounds efficient, but the study reveals a critical flaw: in complex metaproteomic samples, MBR introduces false protein identifications. Specifically, proteins from organisms not actually present in a sample were wrongly transferred from other samples. This means researchers could be confidently reporting the presence of proteins that simply aren't there.

When MBR was disabled, AUC quantification showed a wider dynamic range and higher quantitative accuracy than both MBR-augmented AUC and SpC. It also proved more sensitive at detecting true abundance differences between samples. In other words, MBR-free AUC not only avoids false positives but also captures the true biological signal more faithfully.

This matters because metaproteomics is increasingly used to study host-microbe interactions, disease mechanisms, and therapeutic responses. If the quantification method introduces artifacts, the biological conclusions drawn could be misleading. The study's findings suggest that avoiding MBR in AUC workflows is a simple but powerful way to improve data integrity.

The evaluation was performed on defined complex metaproteomic samples, providing a controlled ground truth. However, the abstract does not specify whether these results will hold for all microbiome types or experimental conditions. Still, for anyone working with metaproteomics, this is a clear signal: when accuracy is paramount, skip the MBR and stick with MBR-free AUC.
