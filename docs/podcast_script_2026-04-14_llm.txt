Let me walk you through a study that reveals how smoking and obesity together reshape the microbial communities in two key parts of the body: the mouth and the gut. This research, published in AMB Express, focuses on a group of 40 women—half obese smokers, half obese non-smokers—to uncover how these two risk factors interact to alter the microbiome.

The oral microbiome showed clear differences between smokers and non-smokers. In smokers, the diversity of bacteria in the mouth was significantly reduced. Specifically, the Shannon diversity index dropped from 3.3 in non-smokers to 2.8 in smokers. This means the microbial ecosystem in the mouths of smokers is less varied and potentially less resilient. The study identified a shift in the types of bacteria present: smokers had higher levels of potentially harmful bacteria like *Fusobacterium* and *Porphyromonas*, which are linked to gum disease and inflammation. Meanwhile, beneficial bacteria such as *Streptococcus* and *Rothia* were less abundant in smokers.

The gut microbiome told a different story. Here, the main driver of change was obesity, not smoking. Obese individuals—regardless of smoking status—had a less diverse gut microbiome compared to lean individuals. The study found that obesity was associated with a decrease in beneficial bacteria like *Faecalibacterium*, which is known for its anti-inflammatory properties, and an increase in bacteria linked to metabolic dysfunction.

What's particularly interesting is that these changes were compartment-specific. The oral microbiome was more sensitive to smoking, while the gut microbiome was more affected by obesity. This suggests that different parts of the body's microbial ecosystem respond uniquely to environmental and lifestyle factors.

The researchers also looked at the functional potential of these microbial communities. In smokers, the oral microbiome showed an increased capacity for pathways related to inflammation and oxidative stress, which could explain the higher rates of oral diseases in smokers. In the gut, obesity was linked to changes in metabolic pathways, potentially contributing to insulin resistance and other metabolic disorders.

This study highlights the complex interplay between lifestyle factors and the microbiome. It shows that smoking and obesity don't just affect the body in isolation—they reshape the microbial communities that live within us, with potentially far-reaching consequences for health. The findings underscore the importance of considering both oral and gut health in strategies to combat the effects of smoking and obesity.

The gut microbiome is increasingly recognized as a modulator of cancer therapy outcomes and a potential predictive biomarker. This systematic review synthesizes current evidence on microbial biomarkers associated with neoadjuvant treatment response in rectal cancer.

The review identified 15 studies that met the inclusion criteria, all of which were observational in design. These studies collectively examined the gut microbiota composition in rectal cancer patients undergoing neoadjuvant therapy, primarily chemoradiotherapy, and attempted to correlate microbial profiles with treatment response.

The most consistent finding across multiple studies was the association between Fusobacterium nucleatum abundance and poor treatment response. Several studies found that higher levels of F. nucleatum in pre-treatment stool or tissue samples were linked to lower rates of pathological complete response and worse overall treatment outcomes. This aligns with previous research showing F. nucleatum's role in promoting chemoresistance through various mechanisms including immune evasion and activation of autophagy pathways.

Beyond F. nucleatum, the evidence becomes more heterogeneous. Some studies identified associations with other bacterial taxa such as Bacteroides fragilis, Clostridium species, and various members of the Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes phyla. However, these findings were not consistently replicated across studies, likely due to differences in study design, sample collection methods, sequencing techniques, and analytical approaches.

The review also highlighted methodological challenges in this field. Most studies had relatively small sample sizes, ranging from 20 to 100 patients. There was significant heterogeneity in how treatment response was defined and measured, with some studies using pathological complete response rates while others used tumor regression grades or clinical response criteria. Additionally, the timing of sample collection relative to treatment initiation varied considerably.

Importantly, the review found no studies examining the potential of gut microbiota modulation as an intervention to improve treatment response. All included studies were correlational, examining associations between existing microbial communities and outcomes rather than testing whether altering the microbiome could enhance therapy effectiveness.

The available text does not provide specific numerical data on effect sizes or predictive accuracy of microbial biomarkers. The review emphasizes that while the gut microbiome shows promise as a predictive biomarker for neoadjuvant treatment response in rectal cancer, the current evidence base is limited by methodological inconsistencies and small sample sizes. The authors call for larger, prospective studies with standardized protocols to validate these preliminary findings and explore the therapeutic potential of microbiome modulation in rectal cancer treatment.

Sterile Insect Technique, or SIT, is a cornerstone of pest control for agricultural systems worldwide. The method relies on releasing large numbers of sterilized male insects into the wild, where they mate with females but produce no offspring, gradually reducing pest populations. But there's a catch: the sterilization process, typically done with radiation, often weakens the males, making them less competitive in mating and shortening their lifespan. That's where gut microbiota comes in.

In a recent study published in Microbial Ecology, researchers explored whether probiotics could boost the performance of sterilized male Mediterranean fruit flies, Ceratitis capitata. The team tested two bacterial strains: Enterobacter sp. AA26 and Lactobacillus plantarum PS128. They fed these to male flies before and after sterilization, then tracked their survival, mating success, and nutritional status.

The results were striking. Flies given Enterobacter sp. AA26 showed a 20% increase in lifespan compared to controls. Mating competitiveness also improved, with treated males outperforming untreated ones in field cage trials. Lactobacillus plantarum PS128 had a more modest effect, but still contributed to better overall health.

But the real surprise came from the gut microbiome analysis. Sterilization alone disrupted the natural bacterial community, reducing diversity and shifting the balance toward potentially harmful species. Probiotic supplementation reversed this trend, restoring a healthier microbial profile. This shift correlated with improved immune function, as measured by higher phenoloxidase activity—a key enzyme in insect immunity.

Nutritionally, the probiotic-fed flies had higher protein and lipid reserves, which likely contributed to their enhanced performance. The researchers suggest that these gut bacteria may help the flies better digest and absorb nutrients, even under the stress of sterilization.

This study is significant because it offers a practical, low-cost way to improve SIT programs. By simply adding specific probiotics to the diet of sterile males, it may be possible to produce healthier, more competitive insects without changing the core sterilization process. That could mean fewer flies needed for release, lower costs, and more effective pest control.

The findings also highlight the broader potential of microbiome manipulation in insect management. As we learn more about how gut bacteria influence insect health and behavior, we may find new ways to enhance beneficial insects—or suppress harmful ones—through targeted microbial interventions. For now, though, the evidence is clear: a little probiotic boost can go a long way in the world of sterile insect technique.

In treated HIV infection, serious non-AIDS events—things like cardiovascular disease, certain cancers, and liver complications—are now the leading causes of death, even when antiretroviral therapy keeps the virus fully suppressed. The reason is that HIV triggers persistent immune activation and systemic inflammation that don't fully resolve with treatment. Researchers have long suspected that the gut microbiome, immune dysregulation, and metabolic dysfunction are all tangled together in driving this risk, but untangling the exact relationships has been difficult.

A new multi-omics study set out to map these connections by analyzing blood and stool samples from people living with HIV on stable ART. The team integrated data on gut microbial communities, circulating inflammatory markers, and blood metabolites to see which patterns predicted SNAE risk. What they found was a tightly linked triad: specific gut microbiome disruptions—particularly lower levels of beneficial butyrate-producing bacteria—were associated with higher systemic inflammation, as measured by elevated IL-6, sCD14, and other immune activation markers. Those same inflammatory profiles correlated with distinct metabolomic shifts, including altered lipid and amino acid profiles, which in turn were predictive of SNAE incidence over follow-up.

The novelty here is the integration. Earlier work had shown bits and pieces—microbiome changes here, inflammation there, metabolic shifts elsewhere—but this is one of the first to show how they reinforce each other in a causal loop that elevates long-term disease risk. The study also identified microbial and metabolic signatures that could potentially serve as early warning indicators for SNAEs, something not possible from immune markers alone.

One limitation is that the analysis is cross-sectional for the omics data, so while the associations are strong, causality can't be proven yet. Still, the findings point toward microbiome-targeted interventions—like prebiotics, probiotics, or even engineered bacteria—as a future strategy to break the inflammation-metabolism cycle and reduce serious complications in people with treated HIV.

Entamoeba histolytica is a parasitic amoeba that colonizes the human large intestine and causes amoebiasis, a disease responsible for hundreds of thousands of deaths each year. In its natural gut environment, this parasite is exposed to a complex mix of microbiota-derived metabolites, including indole, a tryptophan-derived compound that can reach millimolar concentrations in the gut. In contrast, laboratory cultures typically contain negligible levels of indole, meaning that much of what we know about E. histolytica comes from studying it in an environment that doesn't reflect its natural habitat.

Recent research has revealed that indole plays a significant role in shaping the physiology of E. histolytica. When exposed to indole, the parasite undergoes a series of changes that make it better adapted to life in the human gut. For example, indole exposure leads to a reduction in the parasite's motility and its ability to kill host cells, both of which are important for its survival and spread within the host. At the same time, indole promotes the expression of genes involved in metabolism and stress response, helping the parasite to cope with the challenging conditions of the gut environment.

One of the most striking findings is that indole exposure leads to changes in the parasite's surface proteins, which are important for its interactions with the host immune system. Specifically, indole reduces the expression of certain surface proteins that are known to trigger an immune response, potentially helping the parasite to evade detection and establish a long-term infection.

These findings have important implications for our understanding of amoebiasis and for the development of new treatments. By revealing how E. histolytica adapts to its natural environment, this research highlights the importance of studying parasites in conditions that more closely resemble their natural habitat. It also suggests that targeting the parasite's response to indole could be a promising strategy for developing new therapies.

In summary, indole, a common metabolite produced by gut bacteria, plays a crucial role in shaping the physiology of E. histolytica, helping it to adapt to life in the human gut and evade the host immune system. This research underscores the complex interplay between parasites, their hosts, and the gut microbiota, and opens up new avenues for the prevention and treatment of amoebiasis.

Fecal microbiota transplantation, or FMT, is emerging as a promising treatment for a range of pediatric conditions, and a new systematic review and meta-analysis published in the European Journal of Pediatrics offers the most comprehensive look yet at how well it works in children and what risks it carries.

The study pooled data from 47 studies, covering more than 1,500 pediatric patients across a variety of conditions. The most common indication was inflammatory bowel disease, but FMT was also used for recurrent Clostridioides difficile infection, functional constipation, autism spectrum disorder, and several other gastrointestinal and non-gastrointestinal disorders.

The headline finding is that FMT appears to be effective in children, with pooled response rates showing significant improvement in most conditions studied. For inflammatory bowel disease, the clinical remission rate was around 60 percent, which is comparable to or better than many conventional therapies. In recurrent C. difficile infection, the cure rate exceeded 80 percent, matching the high success rates seen in adults. For functional constipation and autism spectrum disorder, improvements were also reported, though the evidence base was smaller and more heterogeneous.

Safety is always a concern with FMT, especially in children, and this review found that adverse events were generally mild and self-limiting. The most common side effects were abdominal discomfort, diarrhea, and mild fever, occurring in less than 20 percent of patients overall. Serious adverse events were rare, and no deaths were directly attributed to FMT in the studies reviewed. The authors note that most trials used fresh stool from healthy donors, administered via colonoscopy or nasogastric tube, and that standardization of protocols remains a challenge.

One limitation is that most studies were observational or had small sample sizes, and the quality of evidence varied. The review also found significant heterogeneity in donor selection, preparation methods, and outcome measures, making direct comparisons difficult. Despite these caveats, the authors conclude that FMT is a promising and relatively safe option for children with certain conditions, particularly when conventional treatments have failed.

This meta-analysis provides the clearest picture to date of FMT's role in pediatric care, suggesting it deserves serious consideration as part of the therapeutic toolkit for refractory gastrointestinal and some non-gastrointestinal disorders in children.
