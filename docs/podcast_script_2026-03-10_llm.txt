Neoantigen cancer vaccines are personalized immunotherapies designed to train the immune system to recognize and attack tumor cells by targeting unique mutations—neoantigens—that arise in a patient's cancer. Unlike traditional vaccines that prevent disease, these therapeutic vaccines aim to treat existing cancer by boosting T-cell responses against tumor-specific antigens.

The process begins with sequencing a patient's tumor DNA and comparing it to normal tissue to identify mutations. Bioinformatics tools then predict which mutated peptides are likely to be presented on the cell surface and recognized by T cells. The most promising candidates are selected, synthesized, and delivered as a vaccine—often as mRNA or peptide formulations—to prime the immune system.

Early clinical trials have shown promise, particularly in patients with high tumor mutational burden, such as those with melanoma or lung cancer. Some studies report objective responses and prolonged progression-free survival in subsets of patients. However, efficacy remains inconsistent, with many patients showing little to no benefit.

Several challenges limit broader success. First, tumor heterogeneity means not all cancer cells express the same neoantigens, allowing resistant clones to survive. Second, the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment can blunt T-cell activity even after successful priming. Third, identifying truly immunogenic neoantigens is difficult—many predicted candidates fail to elicit strong immune responses in practice.

Recent technological advances aim to address these issues. Improved algorithms for neoantigen prediction, better delivery systems like lipid nanoparticles, and combination strategies with checkpoint inhibitors or other immunotherapies are under active investigation. Some researchers are also exploring off-the-shelf vaccines targeting shared neoantigens across patients to reduce manufacturing complexity.

While neoantigen vaccines represent a powerful concept in precision oncology, translating laboratory promise into reliable clinical outcomes remains a work in progress. Continued refinement of antigen selection, vaccine design, and combination approaches will be critical to unlocking their full potential.
