The microbiome regulates host metabolic health and diseases through microbial enzymes. This is the central claim from a review in Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology, which synthesizes how gut microbes influence metabolism via the enzymes they produce.

The review highlights that microbial enzymes are key mediators between the microbiome and host physiology. These enzymes can break down complex dietary compounds that human enzymes cannot process, such as certain fibers, producing short-chain fatty acids like butyrate that regulate energy balance, inflammation, and insulin sensitivity. Other microbial enzymes modify bile acids, influencing lipid metabolism and glucose homeostasis. Some enzymes even produce metabolites that act as signaling molecules, affecting host gene expression and immune responses.

The authors emphasize that while omics studies have mapped associations between microbial composition and metabolic diseases like obesity, type 2 diabetes, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, the mechanistic role of specific microbial enzymes is only now being uncovered. For example, certain bacterial species produce enzymes that degrade mucin, altering gut barrier function and triggering low-grade inflammation linked to metabolic dysfunction. Others generate trimethylamine, which is converted to trimethylamine N-oxide in the liver and associated with cardiovascular risk.

The review also notes that microbial enzymes can be both beneficial and harmful, depending on context. The same enzyme that helps extract energy from food in one individual might contribute to excessive calorie harvest and weight gain in another. This duality complicates therapeutic targeting.

Why this matters is that understanding which microbial enzymes drive metabolic health—or disease—opens new avenues for intervention. Instead of broad-spectrum probiotics or antibiotics, therapies could be designed to modulate specific enzymatic activities. This could mean enzyme inhibitors, engineered microbes that overproduce beneficial enzymes, or dietary strategies that selectively feed enzyme-producing bacteria.

The researchers worked with house flies from two distinct genetic backgrounds—one group adapted to warmer native environments, the other to cooler ones. They raised males from each group under controlled conditions at either 18 degrees Celsius or 29 degrees Celsius, then sequenced the bacterial DNA to see what microbes were present. The big surprise? Temperature had a much stronger effect on the microbiome than the fly's genetic background. Flies raised at the same temperature, regardless of their genotype, ended up with more similar bacterial communities than flies of the same genotype raised at different temperatures. This suggests that the environment—specifically, temperature—can override genetic influences when it comes to shaping the microbiome.

The wild fly data backed this up. Flies from the two collection sites had noticeably different microbiomes, likely due to differences in their local environments, such as the presence of large animals that could serve as microbial sources.

That's interesting because most studies on temperature and microbiomes focus on heat stress, and often find that extreme temperatures cause microbial imbalances. Here, the flies seemed to adjust their microbiomes without tipping into a dysbiotic state, at least not at 18 or 29 degrees Celsius.

This matters because temperature is a critical environmental factor for all animals, including insects like house flies. It also highlights that, for some animals, the environment can be a more powerful driver of microbiome composition than genetics—at least within certain limits.

FAVABEAN and FALAPhyl are two new open-source pipelines designed to make 16S rRNA microbiome data analysis faster, more reproducible, and accessible to researchers without deep bioinformatics expertise. The tools were developed to address a persistent bottleneck in microbiome research: the complexity and incompatibility of existing workflows, which often require extensive manual configuration and troubleshooting.

FAVABEAN, which stands for Fast Amplicon Variant Annotation, Binning, Error correction, and Normalization, is a containerized workflow that automates the entire amplicon sequence variant (ASV) processing pipeline. It integrates quality filtering, denoising, chimera removal, taxonomic classification, and abundance normalization into a single, reproducible pipeline. The use of containerization ensures that the workflow runs identically across different computing environments, eliminating the "it works on my machine" problem that plagues many bioinformatics analyses.

FALAPhyl, short for Fast Amplicon Lineage Analysis and Phylogeny, builds on FAVABEAN's output to generate phylogenetic trees and perform downstream ecological and evolutionary analyses. It includes tools for alpha and beta diversity calculations, differential abundance testing, and visualization of community composition and phylogenetic relationships. Both pipelines are designed to handle large datasets efficiently, making them suitable for studies involving hundreds of samples.

The key innovation here is the combination of automation, reproducibility, and scalability. By packaging complex bioinformatics steps into user-friendly, containerized workflows, FAVABEAN and FALAPhyl lower the barrier to entry for microbiome researchers. This is particularly important as 16S rRNA sequencing becomes more common in fields ranging from ecology to human health.

The available text does not provide specific performance benchmarks or comparisons to existing tools, but the emphasis on reproducibility and ease of use suggests these pipelines could become valuable resources for the microbiome research community. The human gut microbiome is increasingly recognized as a critical reservoir for antibiotic resistance genes, a phenomenon known as the "resistome." Recent research has explored various interventions to reduce colonization with resistant organisms, including fecal microbiota transplantation, selective digestive decontamination, and probiotics, though results remain mixed.

A major finding is the abundance and mobility of latent antibiotic resistance genes within human, animal, and environmental microbiomes. Studies using sequencing-based analyses have shown these genes are not only diverse but also capable of transferring between bacterial species, amplifying the risk of resistance spread. This underscores the gut's role as a transmission hub for resistance determinants.

Fecal microbiota transplantation has emerged as a promising approach for decolonizing multidrug-resistant Enterobacteriaceae and vancomycin-resistant Enterococci. Multiple clinical trials report high decolonization rates, with some studies also demonstrating reductions in antibiotic resistance gene abundance post-transplant. However, success is not guaranteed, and the long-term stability of decolonization remains unclear.

Antibiotic stewardship programs, however, have robust support. Meta-analyses confirm that these programs reduce infections and colonization with resistant bacteria and *Clostridioides difficile*, highlighting the importance of systemic interventions alongside targeted microbiome modulation.

Selective digestive decontamination has also been investigated, though the available text does not provide details on its outcomes. Similarly, while animal models have contributed to understanding probiotic mechanisms, their direct applicability to human decolonization strategies remains uncertain.

In summary, while the gut microbiome is a critical reservoir for antibiotic resistance, interventions to decolonize resistant organisms show variable efficacy. Fecal microbiota transplantation, or FMT, is emerging as a promising therapy for ulcerative colitis, a chronic inflammatory bowel disease. A new systematic review and meta-analysis published in Advances in Therapy has updated the evidence, focusing on how different delivery routes affect both the effectiveness and safety of FMT in the induction phase of ulcerative colitis.

The researchers conducted a comprehensive search across five major databases—PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library, Web of Science, and China National Knowledge Infrastructure—covering studies from their inception through October 2025. They included only randomized controlled trials that compared donor-derived FMT with placebo, sham, or autologous FMT in ulcerative colitis patients. The main outcomes measured were clinical and endoscopic remission at 8 to 12 weeks, with adverse events tracked as a secondary outcome. To assess the impact of delivery method, they performed pre-specified subgroup analyses for five routes: colonoscopy, rectal enema, combined colonoscopy plus enema, nasoduodenal infusion, and oral capsules.

The overall findings were encouraging: FMT significantly improved clinical remission compared to control, with a risk ratio of 1.62 and a narrow confidence interval, indicating a robust effect. Endoscopic remission also improved, though the effect was smaller and less consistent. Importantly, FMT did not increase the risk of adverse events compared to control, suggesting it is safe in the short term.

But the most striking finding was the dramatic variation in efficacy depending on how FMT was delivered. The combination of colonoscopy and rectal enema stood out, producing the largest and most consistent improvements in both clinical and endoscopic remission. Colonoscopy alone also showed significant benefits, while rectal enema alone had a modest effect. Nasoduodenal infusion and oral capsules, on the other hand, did not demonstrate significant benefits over control.

Why does the delivery route matter so much? The authors suggest that routes delivering microbiota directly to the colon—especially when combined—may achieve better engraftment and therapeutic effect. This aligns with biological reasoning: the colon is the primary site of inflammation in ulcerative colitis, so getting the transplanted microbes there efficiently could be key.

It's worth noting that while the safety profile was reassuring, the review only captured short-term adverse events. Long-term safety data are still lacking, and the authors call for more research in this area. Additionally, the optimal number and frequency of FMT sessions, as well as donor selection criteria, remain open questions.

In summary, this updated analysis reinforces that FMT can be an effective and safe option for inducing remission in ulcerative colitis, but its success hinges critically on the delivery method. For now, combining colonoscopy with rectal enema appears to offer the best chance for meaningful clinical benefit. As the field advances, refining these protocols could help bring FMT closer to routine clinical use for ulcerative colitis patients.
