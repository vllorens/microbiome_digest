They promote regulatory T cell differentiation, suppress pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6, and enhance gut barrier integrity by boosting tight junction proteins.

Beyond SCFAs, the review highlights bile acids as another critical class. Gut microbes convert primary bile acids into secondary forms that activate FXR and TGR5 receptors. This signaling improves glucose homeostasis and reduces hepatic inflammation. Secondary bile acids also influence GLP-1 secretion, linking microbial metabolism to insulin sensitivity.

Tryptophan-derived metabolites add another layer. Bacteria metabolize this amino acid into compounds like indole and its derivatives, which activate the aryl hydrocarbon receptor. This pathway dampens inflammatory responses in both the gut and systemic tissues, potentially protecting pancreatic β-cells from immune-mediated damage.

The review also examines trimethylamine N-oxide, a controversial metabolite. While TMAO is linked to cardiovascular risk, its role in diabetes-specific inflammation remains unclear—some studies suggest it exacerbates inflammation, while others indicate protective effects under certain conditions.

What emerges is a complex network where microbial metabolites don't just passively reflect gut health but actively shape immune responses and metabolic control. The evidence points to these compounds as potential therapeutic targets—either through dietary interventions that promote beneficial metabolite production or through direct supplementation. However, the review notes that translating these findings to clinical practice requires addressing individual microbiome variability and understanding how different metabolites interact in the context of existing inflammation and metabolic dysfunction.
