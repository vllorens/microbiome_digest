The human microbiome profoundly influences the host plasma metabolome and health, but most studies have focused on the gut microbiome in isolation. A comprehensive assessment of how microbiomes from multiple body sites jointly shape host metabolism has been lacking. Using data from three independent human cohorts totaling 435 individuals, researchers have now systematically mapped how microbial communities from different body sites interact to influence the chemical composition of human blood.

The study analyzed microbial profiles from six body sites: the gut, oral cavity, skin, nares, vagina, and stool. By integrating these multi-site microbiome data with plasma metabolomic profiles, the team uncovered that microbial interactions across body sites collectively explain a significant portion of variation in blood metabolites. This cross-body site perspective revealed that the gut microbiome alone does not account for the full extent of microbial influence on host metabolism.

Key findings include the identification of specific microbial taxa whose abundance in one body site correlates with metabolite levels in the blood, often in ways that depend on the presence or absence of microbes from other sites. For example, certain oral bacteria were linked to plasma lipid profiles only when specific gut microbes were also present, suggesting synergistic or antagonistic interactions. Similarly, skin and vaginal microbiomes contributed unique signals to blood metabolites, highlighting the systemic nature of microbial-host metabolic crosstalk.

The researchers employed advanced statistical methods to disentangle direct microbial effects from indirect ones mediated through other body sites. This approach revealed that ignoring cross-body site interactions can lead to incomplete or misleading conclusions about microbiome-metabolome relationships. The study also found that these multi-site microbial effects are reproducible across diverse populations, strengthening the case for their biological relevance.

These findings challenge the traditional focus on single-body-site microbiomes and suggest that a holistic, multi-site view is essential for understanding how the microbiome shapes human metabolism. This has implications for personalized medicine, as interventions targeting one microbial community may have unintended effects mediated through interactions with other body sites. The work underscores the need for future studies to consider the full landscape of human-associated microbes when investigating metabolic health and disease.

Trimethylamine N-oxide, or TMAO, is a metabolite produced by gut bacteria when they break down certain dietary compounds like choline and carnitine. It's already known to be linked to cardiovascular disease and some neurological conditions, but its role in spinal cord injury has been unclear—until now.

In this study, researchers used both animal models and cell cultures to investigate how TMAO affects spinal cord injury. They found that TMAO levels spike after spinal cord injury, and that this increase is not just a side effect—it actively worsens the damage.

The key mechanism appears to be through microglia, the brain and spinal cord's resident immune cells. TMAO was shown to activate the NLRP3 inflammasome in microglia. The NLRP3 inflammasome is a multiprotein complex that, when activated, triggers the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-1β and IL-18. In the context of spinal cord injury, this leads to excessive inflammation, which can damage healthy tissue and impede recovery.

In the animal experiments, mice with spinal cord injury that were given TMAO showed larger injury areas, more severe motor deficits, and higher levels of inflammatory markers compared to controls. In cell culture, microglia exposed to TMAO became more reactive and secreted more inflammatory molecules.

The researchers also tested whether blocking the NLRP3 inflammasome could mitigate TMAO's harmful effects. Using a specific inhibitor, they were able to reduce inflammation and improve functional recovery in the injured mice, suggesting that the NLRP3 pathway is central to TMAO's role in worsening spinal cord injury.

This work is significant because it identifies a previously unknown link between gut microbiota-derived metabolites and spinal cord injury pathology. It also points to potential therapeutic strategies—such as targeting the NLRP3 inflammasome or modulating gut microbiota—to reduce inflammation and improve outcomes after spinal cord injury.

The available text does not provide details on the specific dietary sources of TMAO, the exact dosing used in the experiments, or whether the findings translate directly to human spinal cord injury. However, the combination of in vivo and in vitro evidence makes a strong case that TMAO is not just a bystander but an active contributor to neuroinflammation in spinal cord injury.

Critically ill patients often face a triple threat: malnutrition, gut inflammation, and antibiotic-induced dysbiosis. These factors are well-established drivers of poor clinical outcomes. A new study in JCI Insight directly compares two types of enteral nutrition in this context—plant-based enteral nutrition versus the standard artificial enteral nutrition.

The researchers had previously shown that plant-based formulas help preserve a healthy, commensal microbiome. This new work builds on that by testing whether PBEN can actually mitigate the damage caused by antibiotics. They used a mouse model where animals were given antibiotics to induce dysbiosis, then fed either PBEN or AEN for two weeks.

The results were striking. Mice on PBEN maintained a more diverse gut microbiome, with higher levels of beneficial bacteria like Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium. In contrast, AEN-fed mice showed a collapse in microbial diversity and overgrowth of potentially harmful species. This wasn't just a microbiome shift—it translated into measurable health differences.

The study also tracked metabolic markers. This suggests that the type of nutrition not only affects the microbiome but also has downstream effects on metabolism and tissue health.

Importantly, the plant-based formula used was commercially available, meaning these findings could be directly relevant to clinical practice. The researchers note that while more human trials are needed, the data strongly support considering PBEN as a strategy to reduce complications in critically ill patients on antibiotics.

Human milk oligosaccharides, or HMOs, are complex sugars found in breast milk that were long thought to simply feed beneficial gut bacteria. But recent research shows they do far more than that. A comprehensive review in Food & Function reveals that HMOs influence multiple systems in early life, from the gut to the brain.

Structurally, HMOs are the third most abundant solid component in human milk after lactose and fat. Over 200 different HMO structures have been identified, with fucosylated and sialylated forms being the most common. Their composition varies between mothers and even changes during lactation, which suggests they play dynamic roles in infant development.

In the gut, HMOs act as prebiotics, selectively feeding beneficial bacteria like Bifidobacterium longum subspecies infantis. This promotes a healthy microbiome, which in turn supports digestion, nutrient absorption, and protection against pathogens. But the effects don't stop there. HMOs also interact directly with immune cells in the gut lining, modulating inflammation and helping to train the developing immune system. Some studies suggest they can reduce the risk of infections, allergies, and even autoimmune conditions later in life.

The brain is another major target. Certain HMOs, especially those containing sialic acid, are thought to support cognitive development. Sialic acid is a key component of brain gangliosides, which are crucial for neural signaling and synapse formation. Animal studies have shown that HMOs can enhance memory and learning, and there's growing interest in whether they might play a role in preventing neurodevelopmental disorders.

Growth is also influenced. HMOs may help regulate metabolism and energy balance, potentially affecting how infants gain weight and develop. Some research links HMO composition to differences in infant growth trajectories, though the mechanisms are still being unraveled.

What's striking is how interconnected these effects are. A healthy gut microbiome supports immune function, which in turn influences brain development through the gut-brain axis. HMOs sit at the center of this web, shaping multiple aspects of early life health simultaneously.

Most of the evidence comes from observational studies, animal models, and in vitro work. That means while the biological plausibility is strong, direct causal links in humans are still being established.

Still, the breadth of HMO effects—spanning gut, immunity, growth, and brain—marks them as more than just a nutrient. They appear to be active regulators of infant development, with implications for health far beyond early life.
