Bronchopulmonary dysplasia, or BPD, is a chronic lung disease that affects many premature infants, often leading to long-term respiratory problems. While the role of the microbiome in BPD has been suspected, the specific microbial patterns that might drive or predict the disease have remained unclear. A new study now provides compelling evidence that the colonization of both the lung and gut by certain skin-associated bacteria, particularly *Staphylococcus epidermidis*, is linked to the development of BPD in premature infants.

The researchers conducted a longitudinal cohort study, collecting tracheal aspirate samples from the lungs and stool samples from the gut of 39 premature infants during their first month of life. They used shotgun metagenomic sequencing to analyze the microbial taxonomic composition in these samples. BPD diagnosis was made at 36 weeks postmenstrual age using the NICHD severity-based definition.

The key finding was that infants who developed BPD had significantly different microbial communities in both their lungs and guts compared to those who did not develop the disease. Specifically, BPD infants showed an enrichment of skin-associated microbial genera, including *Staphylococcus*, *Corynebacterium*, and *Cutibacterium*. Among these, *Staphylococcus epidermidis* stood out as the most prominent bacterial species shared between the lung and gut microbiomes of BPD infants.

This suggests that the presence and abundance of *S. epidermidis* in both the airway and gut may be a marker or even a contributing factor to BPD development. The study also tracked how these microbial communities changed over time, though the available text does not provide details on the specific temporal patterns observed.

It's important to note that while the study identifies an association between *S. epidermidis* colonization and BPD, it does not establish causation. The mechanisms by which this bacterium might influence lung development or disease progression remain to be determined. Additionally, the study's sample size is relatively small, and further research with larger cohorts will be needed to confirm these findings and explore their clinical implications.

In summary, this research highlights a potential microbial signature—specifically, the enrichment of *Staphylococcus epidermidis* in both the lung and gut—that is associated with the development of bronchopulmonary dysplasia in premature infants. This opens new avenues for understanding the role of the microbiome in BPD and may eventually lead to microbiome-based strategies for prevention or treatment.

The Apis cerana honey microbiome from Northwest Vietnam is emerging as a largely untapped reservoir of biosynthetic novelty, according to a metagenomic study that analyzed bacterial communities associated with natural honey from this region. Researchers identified 366 biosynthetic gene clusters spanning 38 different compound classes, with terpenes, nonribosomal peptide synthetases, and ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides being the most prevalent. What makes this particularly striking is that over 83 percent of these BGCs—304 out of 366—lacked close matches in the MIBiG reference database, signaling a high degree of biosynthetic novelty not previously cataloged.

To prioritize candidates for potential antimicrobial activity, the team computationally screened BGCs from metagenome-assembled genomes. One standout was an azole-containing RiPP BGC from a genome assigned to Atlantibacter hermannii, which was predicted to have strong antibacterial activity with a probability score of 74.5 percent. This kind of computational prioritization is crucial because it helps narrow down which gene clusters are most likely to yield new drug leads from the vast diversity uncovered.

The significance here is twofold. First, it reinforces the idea that natural honey's antimicrobial properties are partly driven by bioactive secondary metabolites from its resident microbes—something that has been observed but not deeply characterized at the genomic level. Second, it positions the honey microbiome as a tractable and underexplored source for novel antimicrobial agents, which is increasingly important given the rise of antibiotic resistance. While the study focused on Apis cerana honey from a specific Vietnamese region, the methods and findings suggest that similar untapped biosynthetic potential could exist in honey microbiomes worldwide. The available text does not provide details on the specific sequencing technology used or the exact bioinformatics tools for BGC identification, but the scale and novelty of the findings point to a robust analytical pipeline.

A new study in the International Journal of Food Microbiology reveals that different strains of the yeast Pichia kudriavzevii have distinct effects on the flavor and microbial makeup of Chinese baijiu, a traditional distilled spirit. Using integrated metabolomics and metagenomics, researchers tracked four P. kudriavzevii strains during fermentation and found that each strain altered metabolite profiles and microbial community dynamics in unique ways. This strain-specific impact suggests that selecting particular yeast variants could fine-tune baijiu's taste and aroma. The findings highlight the importance of intraspecific yeast diversity in fermentation and open the door to more precise control over baijiu production. Source: International Journal of Food Microbiology.

A new expert review in *Expert Opinion on Drug Delivery* highlights promising advances in probiotic delivery strategies aimed at preventing necrotizing enterocolitis in premature infants. NEC remains a life-threatening intestinal disease with mortality rates above 50% in severe cases, and current treatment is limited to supportive care. The review emphasizes that dysbiosis of the gut microbiome plays a central role in NEC pathogenesis, and innovative probiotic formulations—such as microencapsulation and targeted release systems—could improve bacterial survival and colonization in the neonatal gut. These approaches may enhance the protective effects of probiotics, offering a potential breakthrough in reducing NEC incidence and severity in vulnerable newborns.

A new study in the Journal of Applied Microbiology shows that adding prebiotics to fecal microbiota transplantation donors can boost the transfer of beneficial gut microbes linked to metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease, or MASLD. Researchers used an in vitro gut model to test how prebiotic supplementation altered donor microbiota before FMT. The results revealed enhanced engraftment of MASLD-relevant taxa and improved metabolic functions, suggesting that tailoring donor microbiota with prebiotics could make FMT more effective for MASLD treatment. This approach may help overcome the inconsistent engraftment often seen in FMT, offering a promising strategy to restore a healthier gut microbiome and potentially slow MASLD progression.
