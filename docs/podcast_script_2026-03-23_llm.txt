The gut microbiome's role in skin aging is emerging as a critical frontier in longevity research. A new review in GeroScience synthesizes current understanding of how intestinal microbes influence skin health through what's called the gut-skin axis.

The connection works through multiple pathways. First, gut microbes produce metabolites that enter circulation and affect skin physiology. Short-chain fatty acids like butyrate, produced when bacteria ferment dietary fiber, have anti-inflammatory effects that can reduce skin inflammation and support barrier function. The gut microbiome also regulates systemic immune responses, which in turn affect how skin handles environmental stressors like UV radiation and pollution.

As we age, the gut microbiome undergoes significant changes. Diversity decreases, beneficial bacteria decline, and the ratio of pro-inflammatory to anti-inflammatory species shifts. This dysbiosis correlates with increased skin aging markers - reduced elasticity, impaired barrier function, and slower wound healing. The review highlights how oxidative stress, already elevated in aging, is further amplified by gut microbial imbalances that fail to properly regulate antioxidant systems.

The gut-skin axis also involves the gut-brain-skin neuroendocrine pathway. Gut microbes influence neurotransmitter production and stress hormone regulation, which can manifest in skin conditions. Stress-induced changes in gut permeability, often called "leaky gut," allow bacterial products to enter circulation and trigger skin inflammation.

Current interventions being explored include probiotics and prebiotics targeted at skin health, dietary modifications to support beneficial gut bacteria, and even fecal microbiota transplantation. The review notes promising results from studies using specific bacterial strains to improve skin hydration and reduce wrinkles in older adults.

What makes this particularly compelling is the bidirectional nature of the relationship. Just as gut health affects skin, skin conditions can alter the gut microbiome through systemic inflammation and stress responses. This creates potential feedback loops where improving one system benefits the other.

The available text does not provide specific clinical trial data or detailed molecular mechanisms, but the review establishes a framework for understanding how modulating the gut microbiome could become a strategy for promoting healthy skin aging. The authors emphasize that while correlations are strong, causal relationships and optimal intervention strategies remain active areas of investigation.

DNA Traces on the Shroud of Turin: Metagenomics of the 1978 Official Sample Collection

The Turin Shroud has long been a subject of intense scientific and historical scrutiny. Now, a new metagenomic analysis of samples collected in 1978 offers a detailed snapshot of the biological material embedded in the cloth over centuries of handling and exposure.

Researchers performed DNA and metagenomic analyses on threads from the Shroud, alongside radiocarbon dating of two distinct samples from the reliquary. The genetic data revealed a complex mixture of human, microbial, plant, and animal DNA, reflecting both the cloth's origins and its extensive history of contact with people and environments.

Human mitochondrial DNA lineages identified include K1a1b1a, which matches the mitogenome of one of the 1978 sample collectors, as well as H2a2, H1b, and H33. The H33 lineage is particularly notable for its prevalence in the Near East and among Druze populations, hinting at possible geographic connections in the Shroud's history.

The reconstructed microbiome includes microbes commonly found on human skin, archaeal communities adapted to high-salinity conditions, and various fungi, including molds. This microbial profile suggests the Shroud has been exposed to diverse environments and handling over time.

Plant DNA from cultivated species such as carrot, wheat, corn, bananas, and peanuts was detected, alongside animal DNA from domesticated species including cattle, pigs, chickens, dogs, and cats. The presence of Mediterranean endemic red coral adds another layer of biological complexity.

Radiocarbon dating of two threads revealed repair work dated to 1534 CE and 1694 CE, providing concrete evidence for specific conservation interventions in the Shroud's history.

These findings document the Shroud's long-term environmental interactions and the accumulation of biological contaminants, offering a historical record of its preservation and handling. Polylactic acid, or PLA, is one of the most widely used biodegradable bioplastics, but its slow degradation under natural conditions has become a major bottleneck for its environmental sustainability. A new review in Archives of Microbiology maps the latest strategies to accelerate PLA breakdown using microbial consortia, metagenomics, enzyme engineering, and AI-guided approaches.

The core problem is that PLA's ester bonds are stable enough to resist rapid hydrolysis in most environments. Under ambient conditions, it can take years for PLA to degrade, which undermines its promise as a sustainable alternative to petroleum-based plastics. The review highlights how researchers are now combining multiple biological tools to overcome this limitation.

One major advance is the use of microbial consortia—communities of bacteria and fungi that work together to break down PLA more efficiently than single species. These consortia can be sourced from environments rich in biodegradable plastics, such as compost or soil, and then optimized for degradation speed and completeness. Metagenomic screening allows scientists to identify the most effective strains and the enzymes they produce, without needing to culture every organism in the lab.

Enzyme engineering is another key strategy. By modifying the structure of PLA-degrading enzymes, researchers have improved their catalytic efficiency, thermal stability, and substrate specificity. Some engineered enzymes can now function at lower temperatures or in the presence of additives commonly found in commercial PLA products.

The most recent frontier is AI-guided enzyme design. Machine learning models can predict which amino acid changes will enhance enzyme performance, dramatically reducing the trial-and-error phase of protein engineering. This approach has already yielded enzymes with up to ten-fold improvements in degradation rates compared to wild-type variants.

The review also notes that these strategies are being tested across diverse ecosystems—from industrial composting facilities to marine environments—each with unique challenges like temperature, pH, and competing microbial communities. While no single solution has emerged as a universal fix, the combination of consortia, metagenomics, enzyme engineering, and AI is rapidly closing the gap between PLA's promise and its practical biodegradability.
