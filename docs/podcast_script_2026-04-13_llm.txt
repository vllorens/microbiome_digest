Artificial intelligence is now a key player in modern microbiology, enabling high-resolution analyses of genomic, metagenomic, and clinical data for monitoring infectious disease and antimicrobial resistance. The field has moved well beyond traditional statistical methods, with deep learning, transformer-based sequence models, graph neural networks, and multimodal architectures now driving major advances.

In metagenomics, AI is transforming how we analyze complex microbial communities. Deep learning models can now classify species directly from raw sequencing reads without needing reference genomes, which is critical for studying unculturable organisms. Transformer architectures, originally developed for natural language processing, are being adapted to capture long-range dependencies in DNA sequences, improving taxonomic resolution and functional annotation. Graph neural networks are particularly powerful for modeling microbial interactions, allowing researchers to map metabolic networks and predict community dynamics from metagenomic data.

For diagnostics, AI is accelerating pathogen identification and strain typing. Convolutional neural networks can detect antimicrobial resistance genes in sequencing data with accuracy rates exceeding 95% in some studies. These models can process thousands of samples in hours rather than days, enabling rapid outbreak response. Some systems integrate clinical metadata with genomic data to predict disease severity and treatment outcomes, creating what researchers call "multimodal diagnostic pipelines."

Antimicrobial resistance surveillance has perhaps seen the most dramatic impact. AI models can now predict resistance phenotypes from genetic sequences alone, identifying novel resistance mechanisms that traditional methods miss. Machine learning algorithms analyze global surveillance data to forecast resistance trends, helping public health officials allocate resources more effectively. Graph-based approaches map the spread of resistance genes across bacterial populations and geographic regions.

The integration of these AI approaches is creating what the authors describe as a "paradigm shift" in microbiology. Rather than replacing human expertise, these tools are augmenting our ability to process the massive datasets now generated by high-throughput sequencing. The challenge moving forward is ensuring these models are interpretable and validated across diverse populations and environments.
